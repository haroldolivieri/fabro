**Why this matters:**

- Both b and v are labial consonants — frequently confused across languages
- Both target the developer/tech audience

- In search: queries for one will surface the other
- In autocorrect: one will be "corrected" to the other
- Under USPTO "likelihood of confusion" standard: similar marks + overlapping goods/services = likely opposition
- Favro is VC-backed with enterprise clients (Disney, Epic Games) — they have resources and motivation to enforce

## Facto

### Critical Conflict: facto.dev

The domain is registered and live, showing only the word "Facto" with Plausible Analytics tracking. No product, no company, no description. The owner is technically savvy (Plausible is popular among developers). Their intent is unknown — could be building a product, holding for sale, or abandoned after initial registration.

**Why this matters:** facto.dev is the single most natural domain for a developer tool called "Facto," and it is taken by someone with apparent developer intent.

### Critical Conflict: SEO / "de facto" Noise

The Latin phrase "de facto" is one of the most common phrases in English, especially in technology writing. Searching "facto" returns:
- Merriam-Webster/Wikipedia definitions
- Thousands of articles using "de facto standard"
- Various "Facto"-branded companies

**Specific impacts:**
- "facto developer tool" returns no useful results — drowned by "de facto" articles
- "facto CLI" / "facto code review" would take significant SEO investment to rank
- AI search summaries interpret "facto" queries as being about the Latin phrase
- Every tech article mentioning "de facto standard" competes for attention

This is a permanent, structural disadvantage. Tools like "Arc" and "Linear" overcame common-word overlap, but none had to compete with a phrase as ubiquitous as "de facto" in tech writing.

## Assessment

Both names carry meaningful risk, but the risks are different in character:

**Fabro's risk is concentrated:** The Favro conflict is the single dominant issue. If Favro did not exist, Fabro would be a strong choice. But Favro does exist, it targets the same developer audience, and the one-letter distance creates real likelihood-of-confusion exposure under trademark law. This is a binary risk — either Favro challenges the name or they don't.

**Facto's risk is distributed:** No single entity is likely to challenge the name legally, but the cumulative weight of issues is heavy — facto.dev taken, crates.io taken, GitHub org taken, an active 341-star GitHub project with the same name, 10+ companies using "Facto" worldwide, and permanent SEO headwinds from "de facto." These are operational frictions that would persist indefinitely.

### Key questions for the decision:

1. **How important is the .dev domain?** Neither name has it. fabro.sh and facto.sh are the current primary options.
2. **How important is the Rust crate name?** Facto cannot use `facto` on crates.io. Fabro can use `fabro`.
3. **How much does SEO matter at launch?** Facto will be harder to find via search. Fabro will be easier to differentiate in search results.
4. **What is your risk tolerance for trademark disputes?** Fabro has a concentrated legal risk (Favro). Facto has diffuse operational risk.
5. **Is the "de facto standard" marketing angle worth the tradeoffs?** It's a powerful hook, but it comes with a high cost in discoverability.
